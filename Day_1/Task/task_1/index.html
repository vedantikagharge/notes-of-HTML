APJ Abdul Kalam Biography


A. P. J. Abdul Kalam (1931-2015), known as the "Missile Man of India," was a renowned aerospace scientist and the 11th President of India (2002-2007). Born in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, Kalam came from a humble background, but his deep curiosity and love for learning led him to excel in science and technology. He earned a degree in physics from St. Joseph's College, Tiruchirappalli, and later studied aerospace engineering at the Madras Institute of Technology (MIT). Kalam joined the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), where he contributed to India's civilian space program and military missile development. He played a pivotal role in developing the country's ballistic missile and satellite launch vehicle technology, which earned him the title of "Missile Man." Notably, he was a key figure in the Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998, which established India as a nuclear power. Kalam was admired for his simplicity, humility, and dedication to education. After his presidency, he became a beloved figure among the youth, inspiring millions with his vision of India as a developed nation by 2020. He wrote several books, including "Wings of Fire," his autobiography, and "Ignited Minds," which reflected his dream of empowering the younger generation. On July 27, 2015, Kalam passed away while delivering a lecture at the Indian Institute of Management, Shillong. His life and work continue to inspire generations to dream big and serve the nation selflessly.

Early Influences and Education: A. P. J. Abdul Kalam's early life was shaped by his upbringing in a modest household in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu. His father, Jainulabdeen, was a boat owner and an imam of a local mosque, while his mother, Ashiamma, was a devout and caring homemaker. Kalam was the youngest of five siblings, and the family faced financial challenges, but his parents instilled in him the values of discipline, integrity, and hard work. As a child, Kalam was known for his curiosity and love of reading. He would rise early to help his family by distributing newspapers, and he used this opportunity to broaden his knowledge by reading as much as he could. His early schooling took place at the Schwartz Higher Secondary School in Ramanathapuram, where his teachers recognized his potential and encouraged his interest in mathematics and science. After completing his secondary education, Kalam attended St. Joseph’s College, Tiruchirappalli, where he graduated with a degree in physics. His passion for flight and aeronautics then led him to the Madras Institute of Technology (MIT), where he studied aerospace engineering. It was here that Kalam's career trajectory was set—he was determined to work in the field of aviation and contribute to India's development in aeronautics and space.

Career in Science and Technology: At DRDO and ISRO: After graduating from MIT, Kalam joined the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) as a scientist. His early work in the design and development of military helicopters brought him recognition, but his major breakthroughs came after joining the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) in 1969. As the project director for the Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-III), he led the development and successful launch of India's first satellite, Rohini, into Earth's orbit in 1980. This success marked a significant milestone in India's space program and established Kalam as a visionary leader in aerospace technology.

Missile Development Program: Returning to DRDO, Kalam spearheaded the Integrated Guided Missile Development Program (IGMDP), a turning point in India's defense capabilities. Under his leadership, a series of successful missiles were developed, including the Agni and Prithvi missiles, which significantly boosted India's defense technology and self-reliance in missile systems. His leadership and contributions in this area earned him the nickname "Missile Man of India."

Contribution to India’s Nuclear Program: Kalam was a key figure in India's nuclear weapons program, contributing to the development of nuclear-capable missiles. His most notable achievement in this regard was his leadership role during the Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998. These tests established India as a nuclear power and demonstrated its ability to maintain a credible nuclear deterrent. Despite the international sanctions that followed, the tests were seen as a bold assertion of India’s strategic independence, and Kalam’s role was lauded as both a scientific and patriotic triumph.

Presidency and Beyond: The People’s President: In 2002, A. P. J. Abdul Kalam was elected as the 11th President of India, succeeding K. R. Narayanan. His election was supported by both the ruling National Democratic Alliance (NDA) and the opposition Indian National Congress, reflecting his wide appeal across political lines. During his presidency, Kalam remained a non-political figure, emphasizing his role as a servant of the people. His humility, simplicity, and accessibility endeared him to citizens, earning him the title of "People’s President." Throughout his five-year tenure, Kalam focused on areas such as education, youth empowerment, and technology-driven development. He was deeply invested in India's Vision 2020, a roadmap for transforming India into a developed nation by the year 2020. He visited numerous schools and universities, delivering lectures that inspired students to pursue their dreams and contribute to the nation’s growth. His deep connection with the youth made him a revered figure among students, who admired his integrity and vision.

Post-Presidency Work: After leaving office in 2007, Kalam returned to his first love—teaching. He became a visiting professor at various prestigious institutions, including the Indian Institute of Management (IIM) Shillong, IIM Ahmedabad, and IIM Indore. He also served as a chancellor at the Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology (IIST) and worked on several government and private initiatives aimed at leveraging technology for societal development. In this period, Kalam also continued writing extensively. His books, including India 2020: A Vision for the New Millennium, Ignited Minds, Wings of Fire, and Turning Points, became best-sellers and provided a source of motivation for millions of readers. These works focused on his vision for India's future, emphasizing the importance of technological advancements, education, and national development.

Key Initiatives and Ideas: PURA (Providing Urban Amenities in Rural Areas): One of Kalam’s most innovative ideas was the PURA initiative, which aimed at bridging the urban-rural divide in India by providing essential infrastructure and amenities to rural areas. The idea was to develop sustainable and self-sufficient rural regions with access to urban-like facilities such as better education, healthcare, and employment opportunities.

Education for the Youth: Kalam believed that education was the foundation for a nation's progress, and he saw the empowerment of India’s youth as key to achieving his vision of a developed nation. He urged students to be creative, ethical, and committed to nation-building, often telling them: "Dream, dream, dream. Dreams transform into thoughts, and thoughts result in action."

Advocacy for Science and Technology: A staunch advocate of scientific research and technological innovation, Kalam called for greater investment in research and development. He believed that India’s future prosperity depended on harnessing science and technology, not only for defense and industry but also for solving basic problems such as poverty, energy shortages, and environmental sustainability.

Personal Life and Values: Throughout his life, A. P. J. Abdul Kalam remained a man of simplicity. He was known for his frugal lifestyle, often wearing simple clothes and living without much personal wealth or possessions. He was a vegetarian and deeply spiritual, influenced by multiple faiths including Islam and Hinduism. Kalam was also a proponent of maintaining a positive outlook on life, even in the face of challenges. His speeches were often peppered with references to spiritual leaders, poets, and philosophers, reflecting his belief in the power of inspiration and personal integrity.

Legacy and Impact: A. P. J. Abdul Kalam’s legacy is one of humility, service, and relentless pursuit of progress. His contributions to India’s space and missile programs have left an indelible mark on the nation's scientific landscape. But beyond his technical achievements, it is his role as a visionary leader and an inspirational figure that has made him a national icon.

Millions of people, particularly students, continue to be inspired by his speeches, writings, and life story. His vision for a self-reliant, developed India continues to resonate in the minds of many, especially through initiatives like Digital India, Make in India, and Startup India, which align with his dreams of technological empowerment and innovation.

In the years following his death, Kalam’s name has been immortalized in various ways—schools, institutions, awards, and even space centers have been named after him. His birth anniversary, October 15, is celebrated as World Students' Day in honor of his dedication to education and the youth.
